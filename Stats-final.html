Analysis of the Heart Transplant Data

Nathan DeBenedictis

December 2025

Abstract

This analysis examines survival outcomes among patients with advanced heart disease who were evaluated for heart transplantation as part of the Stanford Heart Transplant Study. Survival analysis techniques, including KaplanMeier estimators, log-rank tests, and Cox proportional hazards models, are used to evaluate how transplant status and key clinical covariates influence mortality risk. Survival curves suggest notable differences in outcomes between transplanted and non-transplanted patients, as well as across age and surgery status. Cox models incorporating time-dependent transplant status indicate that age and prior surgery are significant predictors of survival, while transplant status does not exhibit a statistically significant independent effect after adjustment. Model diagnostics support the proportional hazards assumption and reveal no evidence of influential outliers or severe model misspecification. Overall, the results highlight the importance of age and prior surgical history in determining survival outcomes and demonstrate the value of time-dependent survival modeling in clinical research settings.

Introduction

Survival analysis is a statistical framework used to study the time until the occurrence of an event of interest, such as death or disease progression. It is particularly well suited for clinical data, where follow-up times vary across individuals and censoring is common. In this project, we analyze data from the Stanford Heart Transplant Study, which followed patients with severe heart disease who were evaluated for placement on a heart transplant waiting list.

Patients entered the study upon acceptance into the transplant program and were followed until death or the end of the study period. During follow-up, some patients received a donor heart, while others did not. As a result, transplant status may change over time, making it necessary to treat transplantation as a time-dependent covariate rather than a fixed characteristic. The primary outcome of interest is survival time, measured from program entry until death or censoring.

The goal of this analysis is to explore survival patterns in this cohort and to quantify the effects of transplantation and key baseline covariates, such as age and prior surgery, on mortality risk using appropriate survival modeling techniques.

Data Description

The dataset consists of n = 172 patients enrolled in the Stanford heart transplant program. Because the data are recorded in a startstop (counting process) format, individual patients may contribute multiple rows corresponding to different time intervals during follow-up.

Key variables include:

start: beginning of the observation interval

stop: end of the observation interval

event: indicator of death during the interval (1 = death)

age: age at acceptance into the program, centered at 48 years

year: year of acceptance, measured in years after November 1, 1967

surgery: indicator of prior heart surgery

transplant: time-dependent indicator of whether a transplant occurred during the interval

id: patient identifier

Survival times are right-censored for patients who were alive at the end of follow-up. The primary focus of the analysis is to assess how transplantation, age, and prior surgery are associated with survival outcomes.

3.2 Exploratory Data Analysis

Next, we examine relationships between covariates of interest and survival time in the heart transplant cohort. Because the heart dataset uses a startstop (counting process) format with potentially multiple rows per patient, we construct a patient-level dataset (one row per patient) for exploratory plots of total survival time. We focus on clinically meaningful baseline covariates such as transplant status (descriptive grouping), age, and prior surgery, which are hypothesized to influence survival outcomes.

The first boxplot compares survival time by transplant status. Patients who did not receive a transplant exhibit substantially shorter survival times, with a low median survival and a highly right-skewed distribution concentrated near zero. In contrast, patients who received a transplant display a markedly higher median survival time and a much wider interquartile range, indicating both longer survival and greater variability in outcomes. Several extreme outliers are present in the transplant group, corresponding to individuals who survived for extended periods following transplantation. This pronounced separation between the two distributions suggests that heart transplantation is strongly associated with improved survival, even at an exploratory level.

The second boxplot illustrates survival time by age group (Under 60 vs.60+). Patients under the age of 60 tend to have higher median survival times and a broader spread of survival durations compared to older patients. While both age groups exhibit right-skewed distributions with several long-surviving outliers, the older age group shows a lower central tendency and less variability overall. This pattern indicates that younger patients generally experience better survival outcomes, consistent with age being an important risk factor in advanced heart disease.

3.3 Survival Analysis

KaplanMeier (KM) curves are used to explore survival probabilities over time (days). Panel A shows overall survival with no stratification. Panels BD stratify survival curves by baseline groupings such as transplant status (descriptive), age group, and surgery history. These curves provide descriptive comparisons and motivate subsequent modeling.

What is notable from the KaplanMeier curves is the rapid decline in survival probability early in follow-up, with survival dropping below 50% for the overall cohort and several subgroups. Patients who did not receive a transplant and older patients (60+) experience steeper declines in survival, indicating poorer outcomes, while transplanted patients and those with prior surgery maintain higher survival probabilities over time. Because survival falls below 50% relatively early, median survival times are identifiable for many groups; however, to enable consistent comparison across all covariates, we also examine survival probabilities and their confidence intervals at a fixed time point of 1000 days.

The survival probabilities at 1000 days, along with their 95% confidence intervals, provide insight into differences in survival across key patient characteristics in the heart transplant cohort.

For the overall cohort, the estimated survival probability at 1000 days is approximately 20.5%, with a 95% confidence interval of [12.8%, 32.9%], indicating that fewer than one-quarter of patients survive beyond this time point. This reflects the severity of disease in this population and the substantial mortality risk during follow-up.

When survival is examined by transplant status, the estimated survival probability at 1000 days is notably lower, at approximately 6.6% (95% CI: [1.7%, 25.0%]) for patients in the non-transplant descriptive group. The wide confidence interval reflects increased uncertainty due to smaller effective sample sizes and heavy censoring at longer follow-up times. This finding is consistent with the steep decline observed in the KaplanMeier curve for non-transplanted patients and highlights their markedly poorer survival outcomes.

Differences are also observed when stratifying by prior surgery status. Patients without prior surgery have a survival probability of approximately 19.3% at 1000 days (95% CI: [11.4%, 32.6%]), whereas patients with a history of prior surgery exhibit a higher survival probability of approximately 31.2% (95% CI: [13.0%, 74.9%]). Although the confidence interval for the surgery group is wide, likely due to fewer observations at later times, the point estimates suggest improved survival among patients who underwent prior surgery.

Overall, the survival probabilities at 1000 days reinforce the patterns observed in the KaplanMeier curves, demonstrating substantial mortality in the cohort and highlighting clinically meaningful differences by transplant and surgery status. These descriptive findings motivate further modeling using Cox proportional hazards regression to formally quantify covariate effects while accounting for censoring and time-dependent treatment.

The log-rank test was used to compare survival behavior across prior surgery status, and the results reveal a statistically significant difference in survival outcomes between the two groups. Among patients with no prior surgery (N = 87), there were 66 observed deaths, compared to 9 observed deaths among patients who had undergone prior surgery (N = 16). These observed numbers differ from the expected deaths under the null hypothesis of equal survival distributions, with the no-surgery group experiencing more deaths than expected (66 observed vs.58.6 expected) and the surgery group experiencing fewer deaths than expected (9 observed vs.16.4 expected).

This discrepancy between observed and expected events is reflected in the log-rank test statistic (Chisq = 4.4) with 1 degree of freedom, yielding a p-value of 0.04. As a result, the null hypothesis of equal survival distributions between the surgery groups is rejected at the 5% significance level. These results indicate that patients with a history of prior surgery tend to have better survival outcomes compared to those without prior surgery. The KaplanMeier survival curves are consistent with this finding, showing higher survival probabilities over time for patients who underwent prior surgery, thereby supporting the validity of the log-rank comparison in this context.

The Cox proportional hazards model included transplant status, age, and prior surgery, with no transplant and no prior surgery serving as reference categories. Transplant status was not significantly associated with mortality (HR = 1.02, 95% CI: [0.56, 1.86], p = 0.96), indicating no detectable independent effect after adjustment.

Prior surgery was significantly protective, with a hazard ratio of 0.46 (95% CI: [0.23, 0.93], p = 0.03), corresponding to a 53.9% reduction in mortality risk compared to patients without prior surgery.

Age was a significant predictor of survival (HR = 1.03 per year, 95% CI: [1.003, 1.059], p = 0.028), implying a 3.1% increase in hazard for each additional year of age. Comparing a 60-year-old to a 50-year-old patient yields a hazard ratio of 1.36 (95% CI: [1.03, 1.78]), indicating a 35.7% higher risk of death for the older individual.

The model showed moderate discrimination (concordance = 0.60) and was statistically significant overall, as indicated by the likelihood ratio test ( = 10.72, p = 0.01), Wald test (p = 0.02), and score test (p = 0.02). These results identify age and prior surgery as key predictors of survival in this cohort, while transplant status does not show a significant independent association in this model.

The stratified model, which allowed for separate baseline hazards for each age group, and the additive model produced identical AIC values of 594.88, indicating comparable overall model fit. In the stratified specification, age group was accounted for through differing baseline hazard functions rather than an estimated coefficient, allowing the model to flexibly capture age-related differences in underlying risk while maintaining common covariate effects across strata. Within this framework, surgery was associated with a significantly lower mortality risk, with a hazard ratio (HR) of 0.47 (95% CI: [0.23, 0.96]), indicating that patients who underwent surgery experienced approximately a 53% reduction in the hazard of death, holding other covariates constant. In contrast, transplant status was not significantly associated with survival (HR = 1.17, p = 0.59).

The additive model incorporated age group directly as a covariate alongside transplant and surgery. However, the coefficient for the 60+ age group was not estimable, resulting in an undefined hazard ratio. This indicates a lack of sufficient independent variation in age group within the effective risk sets of the counting-process Cox model, once transplant timing and surgery were accounted for. Despite this limitation, the overall model fit, as measured by the likelihood ratio, Wald, and score tests, was similar to that of the stratified model, though none of these tests reached conventional levels of statistical significance. As in the stratified model, surgery remained a statistically significant protective factor, while transplant status did not show a meaningful association with mortality.

The interaction model, which allowed the effect of transplant to vary with age by introducing age as a continuous effect modifier, yielded a lower AIC value of 591.91, suggesting an improved fit relative to the additive and stratified specifications. A likelihood ratio test comparing the additive and interaction models produced a chi-squared statistic of 6.97 with 2 degrees of freedom (p = 0.031), indicating that allowing age-dependent transplant effects significantly improves model fit. This result provides evidence that the relationship between transplant and survival is not constant across ages and that age may modify the effect of transplant on mortality risk.

In summary, while the stratified and additive models provided similar fits to the data, the additive model was limited by the non-estimability of the age-group coefficient. The interaction model demonstrated a statistically significant improvement in fit, suggesting meaningful age-related heterogeneity in covariate effects. Nevertheless, across all specifications, surgery consistently emerged as a significant protective factor, whereas the main effect of transplant alone did not appear to substantially influence survival. These findings highlight the importance of carefully modeling age in the heart dataset and suggest that simple additive age-group effects may be insufficient to capture its role in survival dynamics.

The Schoenfeld residuals plots for age, transplant, and surgery assess the proportional hazards assumption for the Cox model fitted to the heart dataset. If the assumption holds, residuals should show no systematic trend over time and fluctuate randomly around zero.

For age, the residuals are randomly scattered around zero with a relatively flat smoothed curve, indicating no timedependent pattern. This is supported by the non-significant Schoenfeld test ( = 0.90, p = 0.34), suggesting the proportional hazards assumption is reasonable for age.

Similarly, the residuals for transplant show no systematic deviation over time, and the smoothed trend remains flat, consistent with the non-significant test result ( = 0.13, p = 0.72).

The surgery residuals also display random dispersion around zero. Although the smoothed curve shows slight curvature at later times, it remains within the confidence bands, and the Schoenfeld test is non-significant ( = 0.10, p = 0.76).

Finally, the global test yields = 1.38 with 3 degrees of freedom (p = 0.71), providing overall support for the proportional hazards assumption across all covariates.

The Martingale residuals, plotted against age, assess the linearity of ages relationship with the log hazard in the heart dataset. Ideally, these residuals should be evenly distributed around zero with no systematic pattern. In this plot, the residuals are centered around zero but show noticeable skewness and increased dispersion at higher values of age, suggesting departures from linearity. This pattern indicates that the effect of age on the log hazard may not be strictly linear, and that a transformation of age, such as the inclusion of a quadratic term or a spline, could potentially improve model fit.

## Potential Outliers:
##

The deviance residuals plot visualizes how well the Cox proportional hazards model fits the heart dataset and helps identify potential outliers. Deviance residuals measure each observations contribution to the partial likelihood, with larger absolute values indicating greater departures from the models predicted risk.

In this plot, the residuals are largely centered around zero and display a roughly symmetric dispersion across observation indices, suggesting that the model provides an adequate fit for the majority of observations. Importantly, no deviance residuals exceed the 3 threshold, indicating that there are no clear outliers or highly influential observations. Overall, this pattern supports the adequacy of the fitted Cox model and suggests that no individual observations unduly influence the results.

The boxplots compare age, follow-up time, and surgery status between the full heart dataset and the set of potential outliers identified from the deviance residuals. For age, the distribution of the full dataset shows a wide spread with a median near zero (after centering), and there is no distinct or separate distribution for outliers, indicating that age values for these observations are not unusual. The follow-up time comparison similarly shows that the range and median of follow-up times for potential outliers do not meaningfully differ from those observed in the full dataset. Although the full dataset contains several long follow-up times, these do not correspond to a distinct outlier group. For surgery status, nearly all observations in both the full dataset and the outlier subset take the same value, reflecting the binary nature and limited variability of this covariate. Overall, these comparisons suggest that the observations flagged by residual diagnostics do not differ systematically in terms of age, follow-up time, or surgery status. This supports the conclusion that there are no influential outliers driving the Cox model results.

Conclusion

In this project, a Cox proportional hazards model was used to analyze survival in the heart dataset while accounting for timedependent transplant status and key clinical covariates. Diagnostic checks using Schoenfeld, Martingale, and deviance residuals provided no evidence of violations of the proportional hazards assumption, influential outliers, or severe model misspecification. Although age showed signs of possible nonlinearity, the overall model fit remained adequate and stable across multiple specifications. Collectively, these results support the validity of the Cox modeling approach for this dataset and reinforce the importance of careful diagnostic evaluation in survival analysis.
